<h2>The Discovery of the Century: A Giant "Sea Dragon"</h2>
<p>In a groundbreaking discovery that has stunned the scientific community, paleontologists have revealed a near-complete skeleton of an <strong>Ichthyosaur</strong> dating back 180 million years. This massive marine reptile, often nicknamed the "Sea Dragon," was found in a remote limestone quarry, offering a rare glimpse into the Jurassic oceans.</p>

<h3>What is an Ichthyosaur?</h3>
<p>Ichthyosaurs were highly specialized marine reptiles that lived alongside dinosaurs. While they looked like dolphins, they were actually air-breathing reptiles that evolved to live exclusively in the water. They were top predators, with some species reaching lengths of over 25 meters.</p>

<h3>Why is this Discovery Important?</h3>
<p>The newly found fossil is one of the most well-preserved specimens ever discovered. Scientists have noted several key details:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Soft Tissue Preservation:</strong> Hints of skin and blubber impressions provide clues about their thermal regulation.</li>
  <li><strong>Stomach Contents:</strong> Fossilized remains of ancient squid and fish help map the prehistoric food web.</li>
  <li><strong>Evolutionary Timeline:</strong> The specimen fills a critical gap in the transition of Ichthyosaurs from early forms to the giant apex predators of the late Jurassic.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Excavation Process</h3>
<p>The team spent over six months meticulously removing the limestone surrounding the bones. Using advanced 3D scanning technology, they have created a digital twin of the skeleton, allowing researchers worldwide to study the find without risking damage to the original bones.</p>

<h3>Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)</h3>
<div class="faq-section">
  <div class="faq-item">
    <h4>When did Ichthyosaurs go extinct?</h4>
    <p>Ichthyosaurs went extinct approximately 90 million years ago, during the Late Cretaceous period, long before the asteroid that wiped out the terrestrial dinosaurs.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="faq-item">
    <h4>Where can I see the Ichthyosaur fossil?</h4>
    <p>The fossil is currently undergoing stabilization at the National Museum of Natural History and is expected to go on public display in 2027.</p>
